Customer Shopping —
Statistical & Probability Analysis

Personal Data Analysis Project

This project applies descriptive statistics, probability analysis, data visualization, and hypothesis testing to a real customer shopping dataset. Going beyond simple data cleaning, the project uses mathematical and statistical tools to extract meaningful insights about customer purchasing behavior and validate them with formal statistical tests.

Project Overview

Using a customer shopping dataset, this project walks through the complete data analysis pipeline — from raw data inspection and cleaning, through statistical exploration, to probability calculations and hypothesis testing. The goal is to answer real business questions about customer behavior using data-driven methods.

Data Cleaning & Validation

Before any analysis, the dataset was validated and cleaned to ensure data integrity. This included checking for missing values, removing duplicate records, and verifying that all numeric columns contained valid ranges with no negative values or impossible outliers.

  • Loaded dataset and inspected structure using .info(), .shape(), .describe()
  • Checked all columns for null values using .isnull().sum()
  • Removed duplicate records with .drop_duplicates()
  • Verified data integrity — no missing values found after cleaning

Descriptive Statistics

Applied comprehensive descriptive statistics to understand the central tendency and spread of key numerical features: price and quantity.

  • Mean — calculated average price and quantity per transaction
  • Median — identified the midpoint of price distribution
  • Variance & Standard Deviation — measured data spread and consistency
  • Used both Pandas .describe() and manual NumPy calculations for cross-verification

Data Visualization

Created multiple visualizations to communicate statistical findings clearly and identify patterns in the data that numbers alone might not reveal.

  • Price Distribution Histogram — revealed the shape and spread of product pricing
  • Quantity Distribution Histogram — showed purchasing quantity patterns
  • Price Boxplot — identified outliers in the pricing data

Probability Analysis

Applied real probability concepts to derive insights from the data beyond simple averages. Calculated the empirical probability of specific events occurring in the dataset:

  • Calculated P(quantity > 5) — probability that a customer buys more than 5 items per transaction
  • Used proportion-based probability: P(event) = count(event) / total records
  • Applied probability findings to understand high-volume purchasing behavior

Hypothesis Testing

Applied formal statistical hypothesis testing using a one-sample t-test to validate a claim about the average price in the dataset:

H₀ (Null Hypothesis)

The true mean price of products = $50

H₁ (Alternative Hypothesis)

The true mean price of products ≠ $50

Result: Rejected Null Hypothesis ✅

With significance level α = 0.05, the p-value fell below the threshold — confirming that the true mean price is statistically significantly different from $50.

Key Findings

  • Price distribution shows a roughly normal shape with some high-value outliers
  • Most transactions involve low-to-medium quantities — high-volume purchases are rare
  • Boxplot reveals clear pricing outliers that skew the simple average upward
  • Hypothesis test confirms the average price differs significantly from $50 (α = 0.05)
  • Probability analysis provides quantitative insight into customer purchase volume behavior

Most importantly, I learned how to interpret statistical results and translate them into actionable business insights.
